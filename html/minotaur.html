MINOTAUR

In Greek mythology, the Minotaur is a mythical creature portrayed with the head and tail of a bull and the body of a man. He dwelt at the center of the Labyrinth, which was an elaborate maze-like construction. According to tradition, every nine years the people of Athens were compelled by King Minos to choose fourteen young noble citizens (seven men and seven women) to be offered as sacrificial victims to the Minotaur in retribution for the death of Minos's son Androgeos. The Minotaur was eventually slain by hero Theseus, who managed to navigate the labyrinth with the help of a thread offered to him by the King's daughter, Ariadne.

"Birth of the Minotaur"... "Theseus and the Labyrinth"...
The Minotaur was born from a curse placed upon Queen Pasiphaë by Poseidon. She fell in love with a bull sent by the sea god, and from their union, the monstrous Minotaur—a creature with the body of a man and the head of a bull—was born. King Minos, ashamed, ordered the Minotaur to be imprisoned in a labyrinth constructed by Daedalus.
Theseus, prince of Athens, volunteered to be sent as a sacrifice to the Minotaur in Crete. With the help of Princess Ariadne, who gave him a thread to navigate the labyrinth, Theseus killed the Minotaur and led the other prisoners to safety, ending the terror imposed by Minos.
